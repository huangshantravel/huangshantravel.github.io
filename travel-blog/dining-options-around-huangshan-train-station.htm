Dining Options Around Huangshan Train Station

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

Stepping out of the Huangshan Train Station, you are immediately greeted by the crisp, mineral-tinged air of the Anhui highlands. The majestic silhouette of the Yellow Mountains, or Huangshan, looms in the distance, a silent promise of the awe-inspiring journey to come. But before you embark on that trek or continue your voyage, there’s a fundamental, delightful human need to address: hunger. The area around the train station is not merely a transit zone; it’s a dynamic culinary gateway. It’s where the efficiency of modern travel meets the profound, earthy flavors of Huizhou cuisine. This guide is your compass to navigating this flavorful landscape, turning a simple meal stop into an integral part of your Huangshan adventure.

The Culinary Lay of the Land: From Station Steps to Flavorful Streets

The immediate vicinity of the Huangshan Train Station is a study in modern convenience. Built to serve the high-speed rail network, the station itself is sleek, with a spacious plaza. The dining options here are what you’d expect: reliable, fast, and familiar.

The Instant Replenishment Zone: Fast Food & Chains

Directly across from the exit, you’ll find a cluster of familiar international and Chinese fast-food chains. A KFC or a McDonald’s might not scream "authentic travel experience," but after a long journey, their consistency is a comfort. They offer clean restrooms, Wi-Fi, and a quick, calorie-dense boost perfect for travelers heading straight to the Tangkou Town bus station to catch a shuttle to the mountain. This is the realm of practical fueling. You grab a coffee, a burger, or some fried chicken, recalibrate your plans, and move on. It’s food as efficient logistics.

The Local Quick-Serve Hub: Noodles and Dumplings

Venture just a few hundred meters beyond the plaza, and the scene begins to shift. Small, family-run eateries start to appear, their signs adorned with pictures of steaming bowls and baskets. This is where you get your first true taste of the region. Look for shops specializing in dandan mian (noodles with a spicy, numbing sauce) or wonton soup. These meals are swift, satisfying, and often under 30 RMB. They are the working person’s lunch and the savvy traveler’s first foray into local tastes. The atmosphere is no-frills—formica tables, plastic stools, the loud slurping of noodles—a genuine and unfiltered slice of local life.

Venturing Deeper: A Short Ride to Culinary Riches

The real culinary treasures, however, lie a short taxi or DiDi ride away. Within a 10-minute drive, you enter the bustling heart of Tunxi District, home to the world-famous Tunxi Old Street. This journey from the station’s modernity to the old street’s antiquity is a metaphor for the entire Huangshan experience—a bridge between the present and a deeply flavorful past.

Tunxi Old Street: A Feast for the Senses

Walking onto Tunxi Old Street is like stepping into a living museum of Huizhou merchant culture, now repurposed as a gourmand’s paradise. The Ming and Qing-era architecture, with its white walls, dark tiles, and horse-head gables, houses a continuous line of restaurants, food stalls, and specialty shops. The air is fragrant with a dozen competing aromas: the savory smell of braised meats, the sweetness of candied fruits, the distinctive odor of fermented beans.

Here, dining is an immersive activity. You don’t just eat; you graze, you explore, you discover.

Must-Try Huizhou Delicacies on the Street

  • Stinky Mandarin Fish (Chou Guiyu): Do not let the name deter you. This is the iconic dish of Huangshan. Fresh mandarin fish is lightly fermented, creating a pungent aroma that gives way to a uniquely savory, tender, and delicious flavor. Every reputable restaurant on the street will have its own version. It’s a rite of passage.
  • Hairy Tofu (Maodoufu): Another fermented classic. This tofu develops a distinctive "fur" of edible mold through a natural process. It’s then deep-fried and served with a spicy or savory sauce. The exterior is crisp, the interior creamy and complex. Find it at dedicated street stalls where it’s fried fresh.
  • Savory Sesame Cakes (Shao Bing): The perfect handheld snack. These flaky, layered flatbreads are baked in clay ovens and can be stuffed with various fillings—spiced minced pork, pickled vegetables, or a sweet paste. They are warm, filling, and incredibly satisfying.
  • Wucheng Preserved Duck (Wucheng Xiangya): A specialty from a nearby town, this air-dried and smoked duck has a deep, concentrated flavor. It’s often served thinly sliced as a cold appetizer, a perfect accompaniment to a local beer.

From Street Food to Sit-Down Banquets: Choosing Your Restaurant

The dining establishments on and around Tunxi Old Street range from casual snack bars to elegant courtyard restaurants.

The Lively Food Hall & Casual Eateries

For the ultimate choice and energy, find one of the small food halls or clusters of stalls. You can point and choose, assembling a meal from different vendors—a few skewers of grilled mushrooms here, a bowl of liangfen (chilled mung bean jelly) there, finishing with a sweet congee. It’s social, interactive, and perfect for solo travelers or small groups wanting to sample widely.

The Courtyard Restaurant Experience

For a more serene and traditional meal, seek out a restaurant set in a restored Huizhou courtyard. These places often have tables arranged around a small, tranquil open space, perhaps with a miniature rock garden or a gurgling water feature. Here, you can sit down for a proper, multi-course meal. In addition to the stinky fish, order steamed stone chicken (a local wild frog), bamboo shoots with ham (a celebrated local pairing, especially in spring), and stewed pork belly with dried vegetables. The pace is slower, the service attentive, and the ambiance transports you back centuries. It’s the ideal dinner after a day of travel or before your mountain ascent.

Practical Tips for the Culinary Explorer

  • Timing is Everything: The station’s immediate fast-food options are open early until late. Tunxi Old Street is vibrant from late morning until about 10 PM. For a sit-down dinner at a popular courtyard restaurant, showing up by 6:30 PM is wise.
  • The Language of Food: Menus often have pictures. Don’t be afraid to point. Learning a few phrases like "zhe ge" (this one) and "hao chi" (delicious) goes a long way. Most vendors are used to tourists and are patient.
  • The Drink Pairing: Wash down your meal with local Huangshan Maofeng tea, a delicate green tea, or try a bottle of Li Guang beer, a crisp local lager. For the adventurous, sample some Shanlan Wine, a sweet, rice-based alcoholic beverage.
  • Logistics Made Easy: If you’re storing luggage at the station, you can easily make a 3-4 hour trip to Tunxi Old Street for a magnificent lunch or dinner before retrieving your bags and heading to your next destination. Taxis are plentiful and affordable.

The area around Huangshan Train Station masterfully caters to every traveler’s timeline and temperament. It offers the swift, familiar bite for those in a hurry and, just minutes away, a deep, resonant culinary culture for those willing to explore. Your meal here is more than just sustenance; it’s the first or last note in the symphony of your Huangshan journey—a taste of the region’s history, its rugged terrain, and the warm hospitality of its people. So, look beyond the arrival hall signs. Let your curiosity, and your stomach, lead you into the flavorful streets where a truly memorable part of your trip awaits.
